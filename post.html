Measuring SEO Success for Small Businesses: The Comprehensive Guide

In this digital age, a strong online presence is no longer optional — it’s your storefront, your reputation, and your word-of-mouth, all rolled into one. But how do you actually know if your SEO is working? How do you measure what you can’t always see? Let’s unpack that — with examples from the hood and a touch of humour to keep things real.

What is SEO, Really?

Before we measure it, let’s define it. SEO — Search Engine Optimization — is like putting up the flashiest signboard outside your tuck shop. It makes sure that when people search for “cool drinks near me” or “best bunny chow in [your area],” your digital shopfront shows up loud and proud. Without it, you’re basically invisible online. With it? You’re the go-to.

1. Organic Traffic: More Footsteps, More Customers

What to Measure: Use Google Analytics to track how many visitors land on your site from search engines.

Real-Life Example: You optimize your site for “best bunny chow in Soweto,” and boom — traffic spikes. That’s SEO doing the heavy lifting, bringing hungry browsers to your doorstep.

Witty Note: Organic traffic is your digital foot traffic. More clicks, more coins.

2. Keyword Ranking: Own the Search Results

What to Track: Use tools like SEMrush, Moz, or Ahrefs to monitor your position on Google for key phrases.

Real-Life Analogy: Showing up on page one for “cheap snacks near me” is like owning prime shelf space at the local spaza. Page ten? You're practically invisible. Time to rethink that strategy.

3. Conversion Rate: From Clicks to Customers

What to Measure: Track how many visitors take meaningful action — whether that’s buying a product, filling out a form, or booking a service.

Real-Life Example: If 100 people visit your tuck shop site and 20 buy a Coke, that’s a 20% conversion rate. Improve that rate, and you’re not just getting more traffic — you’re getting more money.

Witty Observation: Foot traffic doesn’t pay the bills unless it converts. The same logic applies to your site.

4. Bounce Rate: First Impressions Count

What to Measure: How many people land on your site and immediately leave?

Real-Life Analogy: Imagine someone walks into your shop, frowns, and walks out. That’s a bounce. A high bounce rate means your homepage is either confusing, slow, or unwelcoming. Fix that, and your visitors will stick around longer — and spend.

5. Backlinks: Digital Word-of-Mouth

What to Measure: Use tools like Ahrefs or Moz to monitor how many other sites link to yours, and how trustworthy those sites are.

Real-Life Example: A top food blogger mentions your tuck shop? That backlink is gold. It boosts your site’s authority and signals Google that you’re legit.

Pro Tip: One quality backlink from a respected source beats ten random ones from sketchy directories. Always prioritize credibility.

Your SEO is a Mirror — Polish It Until It Reflects Greatness

Train Delays & JavaScript Delays: My Event Loop Epiphany

Carletonville station. Not exactly a hub of punctuality, is it? More like a place where time goes to take a long, leisurely nap. I was there, weren’t I? Waiting for a train that was probably still deciding whether to grace us with its presence, feeling that familiar simmer of Kasi impatience. My phone was dying, the air was thick with the smell of… well, let's just call it “station aroma,” and my brain was doing its usual acrobatics to avoid boredom.

And that’s when it hit me. This whole damn station, this waiting, this uncertainty – it’s the JavaScript event loop, man! 🤯

The Main Thread: That One Announcer

Think about it. You've got the main railway line, right? That’s your main thread in JavaScript. It can only handle one train – one task – at a time. When the announcer (bless his often-incomprehensible soul) is squawking about Platform 2, he can’t simultaneously be giving a weather report or selling you a dodgy samoosa. One thing at a time. If that announcement for a delayed train to Jozi takes forever, everything else waits. Your code, your website, it just… hangs. Like me, hanging around waiting for this mythical metal snake.

The Callback Queue: All Us Impatient Mzansi Folk

Then there's us. The passengers. We’re the asynchronous operations. We’ve all got things to do, places to be. I need to get this data (my backside onto a train seat). You need that API to respond. Someone else is waiting for a timer to go off so they can stop pretending to read the local paper.

We don't just stand on the main line blocking everything. Nah, we get shunted off to the side, onto the platform. That’s the callback queue. We’re all lined up, tickets (our callback functions) in hand, waiting for our turn once the main line is clear. The guy wanting to buy a cold drink, the aunty needing to ask about the next train to Randfontein, the kid who’s dropped his chips – all queued up, waiting for the main announcer (the event loop) to give them a slot.

The Event Loop: The Station Master Doing His Best (Maybe)

And the Event Loop itself? That’s the station master, or maybe just that one stressed-out official with the clipboard. He’s constantly checking: “Is the main line (main thread) clear? Anything important finished on the platform (callback queue) that needs to get back on the main line?”

So, I’m waiting (an async operation). The train eventually, miraculously, gets signaled it's arriving (the operation completes). My "get on the train" thought (my callback function) gets chucked onto the platform (callback queue). The station master (event loop) sees the main announcement line is free, looks over, sees me ready and waiting, and says, "Right, you! Go!" And *then*, and only then, do I get to move (my callback executes).

If I tried to just barge onto the main line while the announcer was still waffling, it’d be chaos. Derailment. JavaScript errors everywhere. The beauty of it, the maddening, beautiful logic, is that things *wait their turn* without stopping everything else cold. The website can still scroll while it’s fetching that data in the background. I can still curse load-shedding under my breath while the train is "approaching".

The Revelation by the Rails

Suddenly, all those tutorials, all those abstract diagrams of stacks and queues, they just clicked. Right there, amidst the hopeful sighs and the distant rumble that might or might not be a train. It wasn’t about complex computer science first; it was about managing a flow, handling interruptions, and making sure things happen in a sensible order, even when they’re not happening right *now*.

So next time your JavaScript is acting up, or your website feels sluggish, don’t just stare at the code. Go wait for a chronically delayed train in Gauteng. The event loop will make a whole lot more sense. You might even write a blog post about it. Eventually. After the main thread of your journey finally clears.

Who knew a Metrorail delay could debug my brain?

Build Yourself Into a Legend (And Let the Rest Regret Their Role)

Not every door is closed because of bad luck. Some doors are closed because the person you are today isn't the one who deserves to walk through them yet. That's the part they never tell you about becoming.

You can't fake readiness. You can’t tweet your way into evolution. Growth doesn’t care about your intentions. It only answers to transformation. Real, uncomfortable, lonely transformation.

I know because I’ve lived through it — the burn of betrayal, the silence of fake friends, the weight of starting over with nothing but ambition and spite.

I Don’t Do Second Chances Anymore

I used to be forgiving. Maybe too forgiving. I used to believe in reconciliation, in “talking it out,” in “they didn’t mean it.”

Now? I believe in receipts. In red flags. In cutting the cord and keeping it cut. It’s not easy to get on my wrong side, but once you do... get comfortable. You might be staying there a while.

I’ve learned to love from a distance. To mourn people while they’re still alive. It sounds cold, but it’s survival. It's knowing that peace comes with boundaries, and boundaries come with losses. Sometimes permanent ones.

There’s Power in Walking Alone

There’s a version of you that only emerges in solitude. The legendary version. The one that doesn’t need validation, doesn’t chase apologies, doesn’t make space for the people who made you question your worth.

You become a legend in the shadows. You sharpen your vision in silence. You outgrow explanations, and you let your glow-up speak fluent revenge.

Because here's the truth — those who mistreated you don’t get front row seats to your evolution. They don't get to toast your wins or fake-laugh at your success stories. They made their choice. Now you make yours.

The Facade of Family (Just a Touch)

Blood is thicker than water, sure... but so is oil, and try mixing that with your peace. Sometimes, the ones you were raised around are the very ones who can’t stand to see you rise. Not because you’re arrogant, but because your light exposes their shadows.

Don’t beg to be understood in rooms where you were only meant to outgrow the furniture. Let them gossip. Let them guess. Let them grieve your absence in their group chats. You’ve got bigger work to do.

Be Loud With Your Becoming

Document it. Share it. Don’t wait until you’ve “made it” to show up online. Talk while it’s still messy. Share while it still hurts. Let the real ones find you through the cracks. That’s how you build your true tribe — not by pleasing, but by being.

Your people will know. Your people will come. But only when you stop hiding the process to protect the feelings of those who never protected yours.

Build yourself like your future is watching. Because it is.

Mayday: The Day My Mother Died (And I Woke the Hell Up)

24 May. A day the world did forget, but I never will.

They say that time heals. Time, and maybe Rooibos tea. Lies. Time just teaches you how to wear the pain like a frayed old hoodie. Still bloody. Still frayed. But it fits now.

It's one year. One year since my mum died. One year since cancer cleared the ground away from under my already precarious existence. One year since the woman who raised me, struggled for me, gave up bits of herself for me, breathed no longer. And I'm just supposed to what, then? "Move on"? No. I moved with. Icing along alongside the sorrow like a trolley with one wheel that won't turn.

And lo and behold, twelve months later. World's still turning. Electricity still goes out. People still exchange food they didn't cook. And I? I'm launching a legitimate online store called www.whatwouldmamado.store. Go figure.

But let's be real...

If My Mom Was Still Alive, I Wouldn't Own a Store.

Let that sink in.

I wouldn’t be this obsessed, this focused, this unhinged from comfort. I would’ve never watched 600 hours of UI tutorials. Never rebuilt my identity from burnt-out code and broken dreams. I would’ve been fine. Safe. A little too soft. Probably still talking about “trying” to start a brand.

But grief, grief is a boot to the chest.

You either stay down or you get the hell up and make noise.

I stood up, noisily...

How Long Is Mourning Supposed to Last?

A week? A month? Until the uncles stop asking if you've "moved on"?

Grief doesn't get a time sheet. It just clocks in whenever it wants to. 2AM. 5PM. In the Pick n Pay aisle next to the cans of Lucky Star that remind you of Sunday pap.

Grief is a monster like that.

There are days I laugh and work 18 hours straight, like a crazed man...

Then there are nights I cry into a keyboard as though I'm attempting to water the keys...

And then there are days I simply sit. Staring at nothing. Pondering everything...

Mourning doesn't require permission. It simply barrels in like an obnoxious cousin and consumes all your peace.

This Past Year? The Ghetto.

Emotionally? Spiritually? Financially?

Broke. Broken. Yet also... becoming.

Ever asked for food and they spoke to you like you asked for their liver?

Ever borrowed cash and got blueticked faster than your own thoughts?

Ever cried "hungry" and the fridge creaked louder than you?

Ever slept at someone's place knowing your presence caused the walls discomfort?

That year gave that. All of that. No filter. No sugarcoating.

And guess what? I'm grateful.

No seriously. I love the struggle. Not because I idealize it, but because it puts things into perspective...

People. Yourself. The family that only shows up in funeral pictures. The ones who pray for you louder than they ever supported you. The ones who "miss her" but can't even get her middle name right.

I've Never Been Poor. But I've Been Financially Disrespected.

Being poor is one thing. But that spiritual poverty, when people bring you to your lowest hour and deem it adorable fodder for their next brunch chatter?

Whew. That is poverty.

I've gone for walks aware the joke was on me...

I've sat with others who laughed as I spoke and snapped my travails for amusement...

I've constructed on borrowed Wi-Fi, prayed for data packages, and worked harder than sin.

I am not ashamed. I'm hard-worn.

So Why a Store?

Because this is not a store. It's a shrine. A community. A spiritual revolt...

It's where mamas live on, through stories, through stitches, through the love they left behind...

It's where papas speak, really speak. Not just soccer and Spotify, but what it means to raise children when you're still healing the child in you...

Is that not worth celebrating?

It’s where people who’ve ever lost something, a person, a dream, a version of themselves, come to feel seen.

And it’s built by someone who was never supposed to make it...

But did. Is. Will.

Final Tears. Final Words.

This article? My last cry. Not because I’m done mourning, but because I’ve learned how to carry her with me now...

She is the code. The caption. The copy. The customer experience...

She is the WHY of the all-nighters, the design tweaks, the constant marketing hustle.

She did not die for me to fold.

So here's to you, Mama...

Mayday will always hurt. But from now on, it'll also spark legacy.

And to everyone reading this: if you're still grieving, still struggling, still picking up the pieces...

You are not alone. But you are not done.

The Great Cosmic Giggle & My Brain's Bizarre Upgrade...Is it one?

Hear me, I've never been one to shy away from raising an eyebrow at anything that even slightly smells of "miracle" or "divine intervention." My default position on anything spiritual has always been "very skeptical, with a side of 'prove it, boet'." You live long enough in South Africa, you learn to doubt everything...especially things that provide answers...because most of the time, those answers have either a hidden agenda, or a dodgy tender behind them.

So imagine my surprise, and quite frankly, my utter flabbergastation, when things started getting weird... and not "you-find-a-chicken-bone-in-your-shoe" weird. More like "the-universe-is-winking-at-you-and-you're-not-sure-if-it's-a-joke" weird. You get those moments when you're sure you've finally lost it? Welcome to my new address. My brain, which I've always believed was a fairly reliable, if somewhat cynical, piece of hardware, seems to be in the middle of performing some kind of unscheduled firmware update.

Moses, The Burning Bush, & My Sudden Interest in... Interest?

And then there's this. This Moses business. Now, come on, Moses. The staff dude and the water-parting trick. Always seemed a bit of a Sunday school blockbuster to me. But lately, it's not the parting waters that are capturing my interest... it's the burning bush.

Think about it. This man, Moses, he's just going about his sheep-herding business, complaining probably about the drought and the price of lamb, when he sees this bush that's burning. But it's not just burning...it's burning without burning up the bush. Now, my initial, very South African, response would be, "Aweh, someone must have thrown a cigarette butt. Shame, that poor bush." Or, "Is load shedding now impacting vegetation?"

But Moses? He gets interested. He gets curious. He doesn't swipe past it like another TikTok video. He turns to look. And that, my friend, is where it gets freaky. Because in that moment of pure, unadulterated curiosity, he's standing on holy ground. The voice starts talking. The mission gets imparted.

And this, this is the part that's gnawing at me like a persistent township dog. It's whispering something unsettling... "What excites you, what really makes you curious, is usually holy. It's a sign. You're on holy ground when you follow that attraction." My inner cynic is screaming, "Rubbish! It's just a squirrel with a lighter!" But another, smaller voice is saying, "What if... what if that's really it? What if that's the universe poking you with a stick?"

It's scary, really, because it means every stray thought, every deep-dive into a Wikipedia wormhole, every passing fascination could actually be... God. Or something equally unnerving.

Jesus, Daniel, & The Freaky Feeling of Remembering

And it's not just Moses. It's Jesus, the ultimate disruptor, flipping tables and expectations. It's Daniel, chilling with lions like they're oversized kittens. These stories, which once felt like ancient myths, are starting to unlock something. It’s not like I’m learning them for the first time. It feels... more like remembering. Like my brain is downloading files, slowly, painfully, from a hard drive I didn't even know I had. A very, very old hard drive.

And that's the part that seriously freaks me out. It's like finding old photographs in an attic you didn’t even know existed...and realizing they're photos of your family, from before you were born, but the memories are somehow uniquely... yours.

It's unsettling. It's profound. And frankly, it’s a bit hilarious in its sheer absurdity. Am I finally tapping into some ancestral spiritual download, or am I coming down with a really complex form of religious dementia? The jury is still out, and my inner monologue is currently a shouting match between a priest and a stand-up comedian.

But this feeling, this remembering...it’s insistent. It’s a pull that’s more powerful than logic, more relentless than doubt. And it scares the living daylights out of me, because what if this primal memory, this odd interest, this new fascination with what I once mocked as fable... what if it’s the truth? What if, like Moses and his bush, my wondering is actually me, finally, finally standing on holy ground?

How are you walking the sacred whispers in your own life?

Our men, clustered together like stunned, great baboons at the nearest available WiFi node. Their faces illuminated by blue light of a screen, totally immersed in the virtual reality. Try to strike up a conversation, "Bru, what about love? Kindness? This inflation is going to kill us, hey? The potholes are breeding more potholes!" You get a blank stare, an "Eish" mumbled under their breath, maybe a shrug that implies you've just asked for the quadratic formula in isiXhosa. The lights are turned off. The silence is so heavy, you can hear the crickets having a party. We've learned to be spectators of our own lives, deeply interested in artificial drama and pompous glory, while real survival and progress go on about us. Are we so shattered, so emasculated by a system that demands nothing but silent consumption, that we can be passionate about the petty only? Have we become intellectual eunuchs, emotionally castrated by the very distractions we idolize? This is not apathy, it is an intense, bordering-on-pathological disconnection from the very world that is strangling their hearts.

The Great Word Limit: A Post From the Other Side of Thirty-Something (and Diapers)

Come on, friend. In hindsight, from this place on the verge of 40, with a couple little ones surrounding me who require my every moment and word, I can promise you this: we chatter too much. Like, for real, too much. It's not the youthfulness of it; it's the sheer, uncensored quantity of words we spew out on a daily basis, typically about nothing whatsoever of significance. It's trying to navigate rush hour traffic in Jozi, all noise and no real motion, and you're more drained than a dead battery after load shedding.

But what if, just imagine, one day we woke up and found out there was a word limit... 1000 words. That's your daily allowance, no top-ups, no dipping into tomorrow. A virtual countdown, like a very frugal data allocation, ticking away with every syllable. A silent, pervasive reminder of your impending verbal bankruptcy. Imagine the beautiful, eerie silence. The kind you only truly appreciate after an evening of tales at bedtime and incessant queries. Imagine the unprecedented, weighty price of each word, chosen as something worthwhile, laboriously discovered.

The Great Purge of Pointless Prose

And first and foremost, all that wordy rubbish? Gone. Like trying to get a solid internet connection when there's a hurricane above. No more dry summaries of soapie storylines you vaguely remember, or exhaustive deconstructions of someone's dubious lunch choices. The weather? A brief blink, perhaps a "Warm one today, isn't it?" The small talk would go wasted, or perhaps a stripped-down, functional one. "Morning." "Yes." Counter: 2 words. Over. And already, the kids are clamoring for breakfast.

Meetings would transform from rambling, multi-hour sagas that make you yearn to chew your arm away, into tight, mean, laser-like conversation. All of them, keeping their word count like a hawk guarding a field mouse, would omit only the most important, telling points. No room for padding, particularly when you know you're going to have to keep words back in case you need them to explain why the sky is blue for the tenth time. Brainstorming? More like a high-stakes game of verbal chess than a shebeen on a Friday evening. Every word, every move, matters. That single co-worker who dominates every conversation? They'd be sweating bullets, weighing the value of "synergy" against the need to ask about the coffee and determine that they'd still need to economize on words in order to explain quadratic equations to a 9-year-old who's interested.

Relationships would also get a solid shake-down. Arguments would be stripped bare, without over-the-top speeches or the passive-aggressive tango's we've perfected. Each accusation, each defense, direct to the point, like a rugby ball direct hit. "Toilet seat up." "My bad." Counter: 3 words. Job done. Now, the school project. Picture the productivity! And those "I love you's"? They'd be worth more than gold, a valuable commodity, not flung about like spare change. Especially when you consider how few words you have left to tell your children just how much they actually mean.

The Unintended Consequences (Because There Always Are)

There would not be all sunshine and roses, of course? Not everything would be lekker. The world would become a minefield of unspoken annoyances. Like trying to explain the nitty-gritty of load shedding to someone from outside, while simultaneously trying to handle a tantrum toddler. The inability to let off steam, just to blab and sort things out aloud, would mean that there's going to be an epidemic of screaming inwardly. You'd be shouting inside your head louder than the screams of a stadium, but externally, a ghostly look of silence.

Therapists? Out of work. Replaced by silent, sympathetic nods and maybe a mental, "Eish, I feel you." Misunderstandings? They'd skyrocket. Nuance, that delicate butterfly of human communication, would be squashed flat as a pancake beneath the iron heel of having too little to say. Sarcasm would be a hazardous sport, most often misunderstood for genuine sincerity or merely being tactless. Imagine trying to explain complex algorithms or quantum physics to your non-tech family members within only a thousand words while instructing your child on the alphabet. We'd probably invent a new, super-efficient, and horribly blunt system of telepathy. Kind of a brain direct-download, no buffering, pure data.

And don't forget the strategy word-hoarders. Those cunning individuals who judiciously save up their words so that they can have maximum effect, unloading a bucket of eloquence (or raw venom) at exactly midnight, leaving everyone else speechless. The big power move, that one, especially if it's an argument with a teenager.

The Silent Revolution

Ultimately, a thousand-word per day restriction wouldn't just change how we speak; it would radically shift what exactly we treasure in talk. From this viewpoint, with the wisdom of a few more years and the continual reminder of little ears and eyes listening, I can envision it. We'd be left with prioritizing, with really taking into consideration the true meaning and necessity of each and every single word that comes spilling forth. Would we become smarter, more present, more grateful for authentic connection? Or would we just be a society of spastic, angry mutes, expressing ourselves with interpretive dance and aggressive eyebrow wiggles at the cab stand, saving our words for things that truly counted?

My bet? A little of each, like a well-balanced flavor profile... a dash of this, a dash of that. But one thing is for sure: all that endless, senseless talking would finally fall silent. And perhaps, in the profound stillness, we'd finally be able to hear ourselves think. And perhaps, hopefully, we'd realize that some things are not to be said at all, and some are worth every single precious word, like the silence between a child's giggle, or when your child utters his or her first words, "I love you, Dad."

The Sonic Wallpaper of My Skull

Since the start, I've enjoyed "the wrong ear" that my cousins used to call mine. While they were out there dancing to whatever Metro FM was serving, or yelling boy band choruses along the school corridor, I was crouched in a corner somewhere with my Discman, yelling out The Offspring's Self Esteem like a chant: "My friend's got a girlfriend and he despises that bitch."

I wasn't supposed to hear that. That's the catch, music, to me, was never a case of "supposed to." It was a case of honesty. Not the buffed, sterilized, chart-friendly kind... I required music that was uncooked, that smelled of basement gigs and bad life decisions. The kind that was too raw to make the radio, and too real to make best-of lists from people trying to sell toothpaste between songs.

I chose the underground ones because they spoke to me. I wasn't chart-in-love. That wasn't my life. I ached for the late-night confessionals, the rage diary-writing turned into music, the broken person's desperate poetry that continues to strive. Whether it was the soul-punched sound of Alice in Chains, the jazzy sleeplessness of Robert Glasper, or the alien madness of Björk... I chased the ones that spoke to the madness in me. Loud. Soft. Strange.

And I didn’t just carry global chaos within me, I also brought my South African soul with me. Brenda Fassie on Sunday mornings. Zola when I needed to toughen up. Tumi when I needed to see clearly. Thandiswa when I needed to recall home. Even old kwaito rhythms, infested with tsotsi, taught me a thing or two about story. There is rhythm to the way we mourn here. There is song in how we laugh away our bruises. You don't simply hear South African music... you feel it in your gut and in your kneecaps.

No one really understood. "Why don't you just make a normal playlist?" they'd say. But to me, that was like asking someone to write a love note in Arial. No way, man. My playlist was penned in blood, Sharpied on napkins, in a million faces and voices. That was how I coped. The way I made sense of why my brain felt like a haunted house sometimes... why I couldn't just "vibe" like everyone else.

For me, music wasn’t just music. It was medicine. Mirror. Rebellion and comfort at the same time. It wasn't just noise, it was proof I wasn't alone. It was the audio wallpaper of my cranium, layered, louder, and carrying hidden messages that only the freaks could read.

Then one time, everything changed. I started to hear songs in my own head. Lyrics stuck to my lips like lines waiting to be written. I quit looking for songs that told my story... and started to write the ones that do. Somewhere between avoiding radio hits and decoding underground verses, I was summoned. Not by popularity. Not by the charts. By something deeper. Something holy.

That is how the listener became the artist.

Two Altars, One Truth!

I have stood in the smoke of burning incense, watching blood darken the soil as elders murmured the names of those who came before me. I have lifted a calabash to my lips, felt the thick, bitter taste of umqombothi coat my tongue, and swallowed the weight of tradition. I have clapped my hands, stomped my feet, and called on ancestors whose names live only in the mouths of the old.

And then, just as the fire cracked and the meat sizzled, someone started a hymn. “There is power in the blood of the Lamb!” The same hands that held the blade to a goat’s throat now raised themselves in worship. Drunken voices, thick with belief and contradiction, declared that Jesus is the only way.

I grew up here. Between two worlds. Between libations poured for those beneath the soil and prayers whispered to the man who walked on water. Between bones thrown in the dust and a Bible laid on a wooden table. I grew up watching my people give offerings to ancestors and then kneel in church, ashamed of the very rituals they performed the night before.

What are we? A people torn between who we were and who we were forced to become. A people who fear turning our backs on the old ways but also fear hell for not embracing the new. We are a prayer with two tongues, a faith split down the middle, bleeding both ways.

And maybe that’s why, no matter how loud we sing, neither God nor our ancestors answer us the way we wish they would.

The Lesson

I’m not angry… not at you, not at fate, not at the knife you slid between my ribs, slow, deliberate, like a lover’s hand tracing the spine of a dying romance. You only taught me what I needed to know, that trust is a borrowed cigarette, lit in the wind, smoked too fast, burning out before warmth ever settles. I should’ve known better… now I do. Your betrayal wasn’t a wound, just a lesson written in blood, signed with a smirk… and silence. No hard feelings… just a closed door.

Faithful, but Failing

You don’t cheat, congratulations. You kept your hands to yourself, but did you keep your heart open? You stayed, but did you show up? You were present, but did you participate?

Love is not a courtroom where fidelity is your only defense. You think not stepping out makes you step up? No. You can be faithful and still be a storm your partner has to survive.

Because what is loyalty if your love is lazy… if your words cut… if your silence drowns… if your presence feels like absence? What is faith if the only thing you give is yourself, only when it’s convenient, only when it doesn't cost you?

So, I ask again: what is loyalty if it’s not love in action? What is it really worth?
